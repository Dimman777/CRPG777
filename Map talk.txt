# Single-Scale World Mapping System for a CRPG
## Using Ultima VI / VII as the Model, Not the Exception

## Premise

This model assumes the player moves through the world at **one consistent gameplay scale** rather than switching between:

- strategic world map
- regional travel map
- local tactical map

The lesson from **Ultima VI** and **Ultima VII** is that a CRPG world can feel large, coherent, and immersive when:

- towns, wilderness, roads, dungeons, and landmarks all exist in one navigable world space
- the player understands distance physically
- travel happens through actual terrain, not a separate abstract layer
- map chunking exists under the hood for engine reasons, not as a player-facing mode shift

This is the right reference if the goal is:

- strong place-based immersion
- geographic continuity
- physical travel meaning
- visible systemic change in the world itself

---

# Core Principle

## The world is one world

The player should feel that:

- the city is really north of the swamp
- the ruined fort is really on the road to the pass
- the mine is really behind the hills near the river
- danger and politics occupy actual space

The player is not “entering the world map.”
They are already in it.

That means your design should treat the map as:

- a continuous play field
- with different densities of interaction
- but without changing the player’s scale of embodiment

---

# What This Means in Practice

## One player movement scale

Movement should be interpreted consistently across the whole world.

That does **not** mean every area must be equally dense.

It means:

- the same avatar scale applies in town and wilderness
- roads, rivers, buildings, caves, ruins, and borders all fit into one continuous spatial logic
- travel time comes from actual traversal distance, not from jumping to a strategic layer

This preserves:

- immersion
- orientation
- route knowledge
- player memory of place

---

# The Real Use of Chunking

## Chunking is an engine/storage solution

In an Ultima-like model, chunking exists to support:

- loading/unloading map data
- memory efficiency
- spawn management
- AI activation ranges
- object storage
- state persistence
- streaming

It is **not** necessarily a design statement that the player is moving between map scales.

So for your CRPG, the correct interpretation is:

- **Design one continuous world**
- **Implement it as streamed chunks/cells/sectors**
- **Do not expose chunk boundaries to the player unless there is a strong reason**

---

# Recommended World Structure

## One continuous overworld at player scale

The world should contain in the same navigable space:

- settlements
- roads
- rivers
- forests
- mountains
- ruins
- dungeons
- isolated homes
- faction outposts
- patrol routes
- trade traffic
- wilderness encounter areas

Interiors may still be separate maps when necessary, but the exterior world should feel continuous.

---

# High-Level Map Model

## The map is one surface with density bands

Instead of different map scales, think in terms of **content density zones** inside one scale.

### Dense zones
Examples:

- cities
- town centers
- forts
- temple compounds
- market districts
- station hubs
- key quest locations

These have:

- many interactables
- NPC schedules
- multiple buildings
- layered quest content
- short-distance navigation density

### Medium-density zones
Examples:

- village outskirts
- road networks
- farmland
- industrial edges
- forests near settlements
- defended passes

These have:

- moderate encounter density
- some discovery
- route choice
- visible traffic and patrols

### Low-density zones
Examples:

- wilderness expanses
- deep desert
- open plains
- mountain stretches
- marshland
- asteroid-field transit corridors

These have:

- lower object density
- fewer interactables per minute
- stronger emphasis on travel, atmosphere, resource drain, and danger

This keeps one scale without requiring every tile to carry equal authored weight.

---

# Recommended Production Structure

## Build the world from cells, not from separate player map modes

Under the hood, divide the world into cells/chunks.

For example:

- 32x32 tiles per chunk
- 64x64 tiles per chunk
- or another convenient streaming size

Each chunk stores:

- terrain data
- static objects
- interactables
- spawn tables
- faction presence markers
- weather/state overlays
- quest-state deltas
- traffic route metadata

The player should not care about the chunk.
The engine cares about the chunk.

---

# Map Size Philosophy

## Do not chase gigantic raw size for its own sake

The important lesson from old CRPG maps is not just “big map good.”

It is:

- a map feels big when distance matters
- a map feels real when features are spatially coherent
- a map feels memorable when routes and landmarks are learnable
- a map feels playable when density is controlled

A giant world at one scale can easily become dead space.

So the target is not maximum tiles.
The target is **maximum meaningful continuity**.

---

# A Good Single-Scale CRPG World Needs

## 1. Strong landmark spacing

Landmarks must be placed so the player can build a mental map.

Examples:

- shrine on hill
- broken aqueduct
- wreck field
- old watchtower
- forked road with gallows
- sulfur flats near mine entrance
- unique asteroid silhouette near pirate route

This matters more than raw map size.

---

## 2. Routes with identity

Roads and travel corridors should not just connect points.
They should have personality.

Examples:

- safe imperial road with checkpoints
- smuggler trail with blind ravines
- monster-haunted forest path
- pilgrim route with shrines and camps
- old war road with ruined artillery towers
- quiet trade lane that becomes dangerous after faction collapse

The player should learn routes the same way they learn dungeon layouts.

---

## 3. Distance with payoff

If the world is single-scale, distance must be justified.

Distance can produce:

- risk
- changing ecology
- resource pressure
- changing faction control
- social difference
- discovery pacing
- anticipation

Bad distance is empty walking.
Good distance is travel with changing context.

---

# Suggested World Layout Model

## Ring-and-corridor structure

A practical continuous CRPG map often works best when laid out as:

- hubs
- corridors
- wilderness pockets
- edge territories

### Hubs
Major cities, forts, ports, stations

### Corridors
Roads, valleys, coastlines, jump channels, canyon routes

### Wilderness pockets
Off-route spaces with ruins, secrets, monsters, hermits, side quests

### Edge territories
Remote dangerous areas that feel farther from law and support

This gives continuity without requiring uniform density everywhere.

---

# Player Travel Experience

## Travel should remain embodied

The player should usually traverse the world physically.

That gives you:

- ambushes that happen in real locations
- visible patrol patterns
- discovery by sightline and curiosity
- a better sense of “where things are”
- emotional memory tied to place

This is the core advantage of the Ultima-style model.

---

## But physical travel still needs compression tools

A single-scale world does not mean “force the player to manually walk everything forever.”

Good support systems include:

- recall points / gates / ferries / transit beacons
- mounts / vehicles / ships
- discovered-route fast travel
- camp systems
- caravans / public transport
- player bases as routing anchors

These should reduce repetition **after** geography has been learned.

That preserves world reality without turning revisits into friction.

---

# Settlements in a Single-Scale World

## Settlements should occupy real footprint

A city should not just be an icon with a separate map unless necessary.

Instead:

- approach roads lead into outer farms or ruins
- walls and gatehouses occupy real space
- nearby terrain affects city behavior
- district placement reflects history and power

This lets:

- sieges matter spatially
- trade routes feel real
- crime and class geography become visible
- faction presence affect approach and access

---

# Wilderness in a Single-Scale World

## Wilderness cannot just be filler between cities

Its functions are:

- route variation
- atmosphere
- danger
- secret placement
- faction maneuver space
- resource gathering
- encounter staging
- boundary softening between major authored sites

A wilderness band should answer at least one of these questions:

- what lives here?
- who uses this route?
- why does this terrain matter?
- what can happen here that cannot happen in town?

If it answers none, it is probably waste.

---

# Dungeons and Interiors

## Interiors may still be separate maps

A single-scale exterior world does not require every building interior to exist at full exterior scale.

Separate maps are still useful for:

- performance
- density
- verticality
- lighting
- setpiece design
- tactical combat

The key is that the entrance should exist in the world in a physically coherent place.

So the player feels:

- this cave is under that ridge
- this crypt is beneath that chapel
- this station core is inside that complex

This preserves geographic truth even when the interior is instanced.

---

# Faction and Simulation Implications

## The world map itself becomes the visible simulation surface

Since there is no macro travel layer, faction change must appear directly in the navigable world.

Examples:

- patrol banners change on roads
- checkpoints appear or vanish
- caravans reroute
- burned farms remain after raids
- refugees gather near gates
- bridges get fortified
- ruins become occupied
- monster range expands into old roads
- toll stations change ownership
- smuggler routes gain more lookouts

This is powerful because the player literally walks through the consequences.

---

# Recommended Technical Model

## World cells with persistent state overlays

Each chunk/cell should support layered state:

### Base layer
- terrain
- roads
- water
- structures
- vegetation

### Static authored layer
- containers
- doors
- landmarks
- puzzle objects
- permanent NPC homes

### Dynamic simulation layer
- faction controller
- threat rating
- traffic density
- active patrol templates
- local law level
- scarcity/prosperity markers
- weather modifiers
- contamination/corruption/fire state

### Quest delta layer
- destroyed bridge
- opened crypt
- reclaimed fort
- dead NPC
- unlocked shortcut
- placed barricade
- triggered setpiece aftermath

This lets the world stay continuous while still being highly reactive.

---

# Scale Targets

## Practical size for a handcrafted CRPG

For a single-scale CRPG, you want a world that is:

- large enough to feel like a real country/region/system cluster
- small enough to maintain authored density

A good target is not “millions of tiles.”
A good target is a world the player can:

- gradually memorize
- cross in a meaningful amount of time
- revisit without confusion
- see change within

---

## Example structure

A campaign world might contain:

- 4 to 8 major hubs
- 10 to 20 minor settlements or outposts
- 20 to 40 wilderness or route subregions
- 30 to 80 notable side locations
- 40 to 120 interiors/dungeons
- a continuous connective overworld between them

That is already large in practice if the traversal is embodied.

---

# How to Prevent Empty-Map Syndrome

## Use layered density, not constant density

Do not attempt to make every screen equally eventful.

Instead use alternating rhythms:

- safe route stretch
- visual landmark
- fork
- small encounter
- quiet travel
- ruined site
- patrol crossing
- quest location
- settlement edge

This creates momentum without saturation.

---

## Use visibility and anticipation

The player should often see places before reaching them:

- smoke on horizon
- tower silhouette
- station lights
- bridge across canyon
- distant ruins
- road checkpoint torch line
- strange geological formation

This makes travel feel purposeful.

---

## Use recurring spaces with changed state

A single-scale world gets much stronger when the player revisits familiar routes and notices changes.

Examples:

- same road, now militarized
- same village, now starving
- same ruin, now occupied
- same forest path, now corrupted
- same station dock, now closed by strike

This gives continuity real narrative value.

---

# Fast Travel Philosophy

## Do not begin with unrestricted fast travel

In a single-scale world, geography is part of the game.
If the player can instantly skip everything too early, the map loses meaning.

Recommended approach:

### Early game
- mostly physical travel
- limited transport nodes
- route learning matters

### Mid game
- unlock partial network
- ferries, caravans, gates, flight lanes, relay beacons

### Late game
- robust fast travel between known anchors
- but dangerous or unstable areas may still require manual approach

This preserves the value of the world while reducing repetition.

---

# Why This Model Works for a CRPG

## Strengths

- strong immersion
- memorable geography
- visible consequence
- better environmental storytelling
- more meaningful faction presence
- player learns routes and territories naturally
- easier emotional attachment to places

## Weaknesses

- more demanding content layout
- easy to accidentally create dead space
- traversal repetition can become a problem
- more complex streaming/persistence requirements

So this model works best when the world is designed around **meaningful continuity**, not mere scale.

---

# Final Design Rule

## Build one world, not three disguised as one

If using Ultima VI / VII as the reference point, the correct lesson is:

- keep the player grounded in one navigable world scale
- let technical chunking stay technical
- use density bands, travel tools, and persistent state to manage scope
- make geography itself part of role-playing, questing, and simulation

The player should feel:
“I know this world because I have moved through it.”

That is the real power of the model.

---

# Short Version

## Recommended mapping model

- one continuous player-scale overworld
- chunked internally for streaming and persistence
- settlements, roads, wilderness, and landmarks all exist in the same navigable space
- interiors can be instanced, but entrances remain geographically coherent
- density varies by zone, but player scale does not
- faction/state change appears directly in the world
- fast travel unlocks later as a convenience, not as the primary structure

This is the cleanest CRPG interpretation of the Ultima VI / VII approach.
